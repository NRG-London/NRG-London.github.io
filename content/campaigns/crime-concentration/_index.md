## The Law of Crime Concentration

Crime is not spread evenly. Wherever researchers have looked, they have found the same pattern: **a small share of places account for a large share of offences**.

The finding traces back to Lawrence Sherman's work in Minneapolis in 1989, which showed that around **3% of addresses generated more than half** of all calls for police service. David Weisburd later formalised it as the "**law of crime concentration at places**", proposing that roughly 5% of street segments in a city account for about half of its crime.

This is more than a curiosity for two reasons.

First: stability. **The hot spots do not wander**. Weisburd's Seattle study tracked street segments over sixteen years and found the high-crime places largely stayed high-crime, and the quiet ones stayed quiet, even as the city's overall crime rate fell substantially. Concentration is a fact about particular places, not a statistical accident that reshuffles each year.

The second is that it holds at a very fine grain. Concentration is much sharper at the level of the street segment or the individual address than at the level of a ward or a borough. The maps here work at ward level, so **what you are seeing actually understates the true concentration** rather than exaggerating it.

**This matters for what police do.** If offending were evenly spread, patrol would be a numbers game and enforcement would be whack-a-mole. Because it is concentrated and stable, resource can be targeted. **The evidence on hot spot policing is unusually strong for criminology**: systematic reviews consistently find that targeting these areas gives you big crime reductions. And contrary to what you might imagine, pushing down the crime hotspot doesn't work like a half-inflated balloon, popping up somewhere else. Crime researchers more often find a diffusion of benefit into surrounding streets than displacement into them.

This is exactly the principle behind my [February 2025 report on tackling phone theft](/campaigns/phone-theft/). Target the hotspots, the West End in particular, and crime will come down. That is exactly what has now happened.
